ENG VS. EFP: WHAT'S THE DIFFERENCE?

Both terms get thrown around loosely on set. Here's what actually separates an ENG crew from an EFP crew, and a simple way to know which one to ask for.

Kyle ZuelkeJune 23, 2026 · 5 min read

ENG and EFP both describe field production — shooting outside a studio — but they're built for different jobs. An ENG crew is fast and lean: a camera operator, minimal gear, in and out quickly, built for news-style coverage where the priority is capturing something as it happens. An EFP crew is more deliberate: more setup time, more lighting, sometimes more people, built around a planned shot list rather than reacting on the fly. Neither one is "better" — they're suited to different shoots, and knowing which your project actually needs saves time and money before a call sheet ever gets built.

ENG: built for speed

ENG, short for Electronic News Gathering, is the production style behind interviews, b-roll, corporate updates, and same-day event coverage. The crew is small, usually a camera operator with an audio kit, sometimes joined by a second person. There's little to no lighting setup beyond what's needed to make a subject look clean on camera. The whole model is optimized around turnaround time — shoot today, edit tonight, deliver tomorrow. A standard ENG crew with camera, audio kit, and lighting starts at $1,950 per 10-hour day in Las Vegas. For a deeper look at what ENG means on its own, see what is an ENG crew.

EFP: built for control

EFP, or Electronic Field Production, is what happens when a shoot needs more control over the final image than ENG allows. That might mean a proper lighting setup for an interview, a dedicated audio technician instead of a camera operator handling both jobs, or multiple camera positions covering an event from more than one angle. EFP crews plan more before shoot day — a shot list, a lighting plan, sometimes a location scout — because the goal is a specific, controlled look rather than fast reactive coverage. Full ENG/EFP packages with an audio tech and additional gear start at $3,000 per 10-hour day. Details on both tiers are on the ENG/EFP services page.

Crew size and gear: what actually shows up

The clearest practical difference between ENG and EFP is what actually rolls up to the location. A typical ENG call is one camera operator, one broadcast-grade camera body — often a Sony FX9 or similar — and an audio kit that clips onto a subject in under a minute. A typical EFP call adds a dedicated audio technician, a compact lighting kit, and sometimes a second camera for a reaction shot or a wider establishing angle. Crew size scales with control: the more a producer needs to shape lighting, composition, and coverage in advance, the more people it takes to execute on the day. That's not a knock on ENG — a two-person crew moving fast is exactly the right tool for the job it's built for. It just isn't the same job as a lit, multi-angle interview shoot, and pricing a shoot correctly starts with knowing which one you're actually booking.

A simple way to know which one you need

Ask two questions about the shoot. First: does this need to happen fast, with minimal disruption to whatever's already going on — an interview between someone's meetings, a quick reaction shot, a news-style hit? That's ENG. Second: does this need to look a specific, polished way, with lighting and composition planned in advance — a branded interview series, a corporate video with a defined visual style, an event with more than one camera angle? That's EFP. Most shoots fall clearly into one category once framed this way, even when the person booking didn't know the terminology going in.

Where the line gets blurry

In practice, ENG and EFP aren't a hard switch — they're a spectrum, and a lot of real shoots sit somewhere in the middle. An ENG shoot might add a small light kit without becoming a full EFP production. An EFP shoot might still move fast if the crew is experienced enough to set up quickly. The distinction matters most for budgeting and scheduling purposes: EFP shoots generally need more lead time and a bigger crew, while ENG shoots can often be booked same-day. If your timeline is genuinely tight, booking a video crew on short notice covers what's realistic to expect.

A same-day interview for a news-style segment is usually pure ENG — no lighting kit, no shot list, just a camera operator reacting to the conversation as it happens. A branded interview series destined for a company's website or YouTube channel leans closer to EFP, even if it only takes half a day, because visual consistency across episodes justifies a lighting setup and a locked camera position. Producers who aren't sure which side of that line their project falls on are generally better off describing the shoot in plain terms and letting the crew recommend a format, rather than guessing at the terminology upfront.

Multi-camera is a separate category

It's worth noting that neither ENG nor EFP typically describes multi-camera, switched production — that's its own category, used for live or live-to-tape shows that need several synced cameras feeding a switcher in real time. If your project involves cutting between multiple live angles rather than a single camera's coverage, see single-camera vs. multi-camera production for that comparison instead.

The bottom line

None of these categories are rigid, and an experienced crew will adjust the plan once they understand what a shoot actually needs, regardless of which label gets used to book it. But walking into that first conversation with the right vocabulary — ENG for speed and simplicity, EFP for control and planning, multi-camera for anything cutting live between angles — makes the conversation faster and the resulting quote more accurate. If it's still not obvious which category your project falls into, describing the shoot in plain language on the contact page works just as well; matching it to the right crew model is part of the quote process either way.
