WHY LAS VEGAS IS A HUB FOR LIVE EVENT PRODUCTION

Las Vegas didn't become a live production market by accident — it's a function of venue density, a packed sports and convention calendar, and crews who've been working this city long enough to know it cold.

Kyle ZuelkeJune 30, 2026 · 6 min read

Las Vegas has quietly become one of the busiest live production markets in the country, and it's not just the neon. It's the sheer number of major venues packed into a small footprint, the density of sporting events and conventions that fill them week after week, and a pool of crews who've worked this city long enough to move fast in it. Producers flying in for a single event often don't realize how much of the groundwork — venue access, load-in logistics, local vendor relationships — is already solved simply by hiring people who work here regularly.

A convention capital first

Long before it was a sports town, Las Vegas was a convention town, and it still hosts more trade shows than almost any other city in the country. The Las Vegas Convention Center alone runs a packed calendar of tech, medical, automotive, and consumer events, each one bringing its own production needs — keynote coverage, exhibitor video, social content, live streams for attendees who couldn't make the trip. That volume built a local production infrastructure — camera operators, editors, gear houses, and crew — that didn't exist at this scale in most other cities. For more on what that specific kind of work looks like, see trade show video production in Las Vegas.

A major sports market now

What changed the city's production landscape more recently is professional sports. The Raiders' move to Allegiant Stadium, the Golden Knights and now the Aces at T-Mobile Arena and Michelob Ultra Arena, and a growing calendar of marquee one-off events have turned Las Vegas into a recurring stop for national broadcasters. Z Squared crewed Super Bowl LVIII at Allegiant Stadium with a 5-camera package and fiber transmission for NFL Network, along with NBA All-Star coverage, a Boston Celtics media day, and ESPN's WNBA Aces coverage — work that simply wasn't happening in this city at this volume a decade ago. UFC and WWE have also kept a steady production presence here for years, on top of that.

A market that rarely slows down

What makes Las Vegas unusual isn't any single event — it's the density of the calendar. Convention season runs heavy through the first half of the year, major fights and residencies run year-round, and football, basketball, and hockey now fill out the fall and winter. Overlap it all in a city small enough that most of these venues sit within twenty minutes of each other, and the result is a production market that stays busy in a way most cities don't. A crew based anywhere else has to fly in, scout, and figure out logistics fresh for every booking. A crew based here is often already familiar with the building before the call ever comes in.

The venues that make it possible

None of this works without the venues themselves, and Las Vegas has an unusual concentration of them within a short drive of each other:

  • Allegiant Stadium
  • T-Mobile Arena
  • Michelob Ultra Arena
  • Las Vegas Convention Center
  • UFC Apex

A crew that regularly works all five of these doesn't need a location scout to explain where the loading dock is or how far the truck can park from the tunnel. That familiarity compounds over time, and it's a big part of why local crews get rebooked for national broadcasts instead of flying a team in from another market.

Why local experience actually matters here

Every one of these venues has its own quirks — power drops in different spots, different rules for cable runs, different security and credentialing processes for a stadium versus a convention hall versus a fight venue. A crew that's worked here for years has already made the mistakes that cost time on someone else's shoot. Owner and lead DP Kyle Zuelke moved to Las Vegas in 2003 to join KLAS-TV's I-Team, spent 13 years shooting daily news and sports across the Valley, and founded Z Squared in 2016 — which means this familiarity isn't a marketing line, it's two decades of actually being on these cameras in this city. Read more about that background on the about page.

That kind of tenure also shows up in smaller ways that only matter once a shoot is underway: knowing which entrance actually gets a truck closest to load-in, which credentialing office to call first, which hours a venue is realistically accessible before an event. None of that is glamorous, and none of it shows up on a call sheet, but it's the difference between a crew that spends the first hour of a shoot figuring out the building and one that starts rolling on time.

What this means for producers booking Las Vegas

For a producer flying in from another market, the practical upshot is simple: a local crew removes a layer of risk. Venue access, parking, load-in timing, permit rules, even knowing which convention hall gets better afternoon light — all of it gets handled by people who've done it before, rather than being solved live on shoot day. That matters more in Las Vegas than in most cities, precisely because there's so much production volume moving through so few venues. A crew that already knows the building is a genuine time and cost advantage, not just a convenience.
