TRADE SHOW VIDEO PRODUCTION IN LAS VEGAS

Las Vegas hosts more trade shows than any city in America. Here's what to know about producing video on the show floor — and why it's a different job than a studio shoot.

Kyle ZuelkeMay 19, 2026 · 4 min read

Las Vegas hosts more trade shows than any other city in the country, and a large share of the video production work in this market happens on a convention floor rather than a soundstage. Booth interviews, product demos, sizzle reels, and same-day recap videos are their own category of shoot, with their own set of problems that a typical studio production never has to solve.

Why does so much video production happen on Vegas show floors?

Between the Las Vegas Convention Center and the massive convention spaces built into Strip resort properties, this city hosts an enormous volume of national trade shows every year, across nearly every industry imaginable. Exhibitors fly in with a few days on the floor and one shot at capturing content that represents their brand — which means the video crew has to be fast, self-sufficient, and comfortable working in a noisy, crowded, unpredictable environment.

What kind of video do exhibitors actually need?

Trade show video requests tend to cluster into a few recurring types: booth interviews with executives or product leads, short product demo walk-throughs, attendee and floor-atmosphere b-roll for a highlight reel, and sponsor or partner recap videos that get turned around for social media before the show even closes. Some clients also want a longer sizzle reel combining all of it for use after the show — in sales decks, on a website, or in next year's exhibitor pitch. Knowing which of these a client needs before the crew shows up changes how the day gets shot; a quick social clip and a polished sizzle reel call for different coverage and different pacing on set.

What's different about shooting booth interviews?

A booth interview isn't a controlled studio sit-down. There's foot traffic in the background, a neighboring booth running its own audio, and often only a few square feet of usable frame that doesn't show a competitor's signage. Getting a clean interview means working fast to find the right angle, using directional audio to isolate the speaker's voice, and often shooting in short, tightly blocked segments rather than one long uninterrupted take.

How do you deal with show floor noise?

Noise is the single biggest technical challenge on a convention floor. PA announcements, neighboring booth demos, and general crowd volume all compete with your subject's voice. The practical fixes are close-mic'd audio — lav mics or a boom held tight — combined with picking interview times and locations that avoid the loudest stretches of the day. A quiet corner or a dedicated media room, when the show provides one, is worth prioritizing over a more visually interesting but noisier spot.

What about lighting on the show floor?

Convention hall lighting is built for the room, not for camera. It's often flat, mixed in color temperature, and inconsistent from one hall to the next. A crew that brings its own portable lighting — even something simple, like an LED panel and a bounce — can make the difference between usable footage and a washed-out, uneven booth interview. This is part of why a full ENG-style kit, not just a camera body, matters for this kind of shoot.

What access and badging logistics should you plan for?

Show floors run on credentials. A few things worth confirming before your crew arrives:

  • Whether the venue requires a separate media or vendor badge for camera equipment
  • Load-in times and which dock or entrance handles gear
  • Whether a tripod or lighting stand needs prior approval on a crowded floor
  • Any restricted hours for filming near competitor booths

None of this is complicated, but it's the kind of detail that causes real delays if it's sorted out on arrival instead of ahead of time. A quick call to the venue's exhibitor services desk a day or two ahead of the show usually answers all of it at once.

How big a crew does a trade show shoot actually need?

Most booth coverage doesn't need a large footprint. A single camera operator handling both camera and audio can cover interviews and b-roll for a standard booth over the course of a show day. Larger exhibitors running back-to-back scheduled interviews, or covering a keynote plus the booth simultaneously, benefit from a second operator so one crew isn't running between locations on the floor. It's a similar calculation to any other shoot — the crew size should match what's actually being covered, not the size of the show itself.

Can you actually get a same-day recap video?

Often, yes — and it's one of the more valuable deliverables for a trade show client, since a next-day recap loses most of its marketing value. A same-day turnaround depends on shooting efficiently throughout the day and having an edit workflow that doesn't wait until the show floor closes to start. This overlaps closely with our brand and social video work, which is built around fast-turnaround deliverables rather than long post-production timelines.

If your show falls outside a normal planning window, our post on booking a video crew on short notice covers what's realistic same-day versus what needs lead time. And for a broader look at how this market handles live event work generally, why Las Vegas is a live event production hub is a good next read.

Whatever the show, the fundamentals stay the same: fast setup, clean audio despite the noise, lighting that doesn't rely on the house rig, and an edit turnaround that respects how quickly trade show content goes stale.
