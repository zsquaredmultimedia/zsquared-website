WHAT AFFECTS A CAMERA OPERATOR'S DAY RATE?

Day rates for Las Vegas camera operators vary widely. Here are the real factors that drive the price up or down, and why "how much for a camera operator" doesn't have one answer.

Kyle ZuelkeJune 2, 2026 · 5 min read

Ask five production companies what a camera operator costs in Las Vegas and you'll get five different numbers, because the question is hiding several variables inside it. A standard ENG crew with camera, audio kit, and lighting starts at $1,950 per 10-hour day in Las Vegas. A full ENG/EFP package with an audio tech and additional gear starts at $3,000 per 10-hour day. Everything past that baseline — crew size, gear, timing, location — is what actually moves a camera operator day rate from one shoot to the next.

What's actually included in a standard day rate?

The $1,950 figure covers a single camera operator, a camera package, an audio kit, and basic lighting for a 10-hour day. That's the floor for a professional, insured, broadcast-capable shoot in this market — not a rate for a bare operator showing up with just a body and a lens. Once you add a dedicated audio technician and a larger gear list, you're in full ENG/EFP territory, which is where the $3,000 figure starts.

Does crew size change the rate?

Directly, yes. A single camera operator handling both camera and basic audio is the cheapest configuration that still produces broadcast-usable footage. Add a second camera operator for multi-angle coverage, or a dedicated audio tech so the primary operator isn't splitting attention, and the day rate climbs accordingly — because you're now paying for two or three skilled people instead of one. This is also the core question behind choosing between a single ENG crew and a full EFP package: it's rarely about which is "better," it's about how many hands the shoot actually needs.

How much does gear complexity move the price?

A shoot on a Sony FX9 with a full lighting kit, wireless lav mics, and a shotgun on a boom is a different equipment footprint than a run-and-gun interview on a lighter body. More gear means more setup time, more insurance exposure, and more truck space — all of which factor into the rate. This is one reason "camera operator" day rate questions can't be answered with a single number until someone knows the gear list.

What counts as a standard day versus overtime?

Both published rates are built around a 10-hour day. That's a deliberate standard in broadcast and event work because it's long enough to cover setup, the shoot itself, and strike without constant clock-watching. Go past 10 hours and you're into overtime territory, which costs more per hour than the base rate — and a second consecutive overnight or an unusually early call can push into doubletime depending on how the schedule is built. Producers who plan a realistic day length up front avoid most overtime surprises entirely.

Does booking on short notice cost more?

Sometimes. A same-day or next-day booking can carry a premium depending on crew availability and how much lead time there is to confirm gear and personnel. It's not a punitive rush fee so much as a reflection of what it takes to reshuffle a schedule on short notice. We cover the mechanics of this in more detail in how to book a video crew on short notice — the honest version is that some things move fast and some genuinely need lead time.

Does travel affect the day rate?

Yes, in a straightforward way: a shoot in Henderson or on the Strip doesn't carry the same logistics as one that requires driving gear to Los Angeles or flying a crew out of state. Mileage, drive time, and any overnight lodging for a multi-day shoot outside the Vegas Valley get factored in separately from the base day rate.

Why do live and multi-cam shows get quoted separately?

Live transmission and multi-cam shows fall outside the standard day-rate structure entirely, and that's not an attempt to be vague — it's because the variables multiply fast. A multi-cam build might run four cameras through a small switcher or fourteen cameras through something like an ATEM Constellation, a NewTek Tricaster, or vMix, and each of those configurations needs a different number of operators, a different amount of cable and switching gear, and a different setup timeline. Add a live transmission path — fiber, TVU, RTMP, or SRT — and now there's an engineer and a signal-testing window on top of the camera crew. None of that fits cleanly into a flat per-day number, which is why those shows get quoted against the specific event rather than against a published rate.

Single operator or multi-op — which do you actually need?

A lot of budget confusion comes from not knowing which crew size a shoot actually requires. A single-camera interview doesn't need the same footprint as a multi-cam panel discussion or a live event. Some quick guidelines:

  • Single sit-down interviews or b-roll: one operator, standard rate
  • Interviews with a dedicated audio tech: full ENG/EFP rate
  • Multi-angle events or live shows: multi-cam pricing, quoted per event
  • Any live transmission component (fiber, TVU, RTMP, SRT): quoted per event

For a deeper look at how single-camera and multi-camera setups differ operationally, our post on single-camera vs. multi-camera production walks through the decision in more detail. And if you're still weighing whether a freelancer or a full crew fits your project better, freelance camera operator vs. production company is a useful next read.

None of this is meant to replace an actual quote — it's meant to explain why quotes differ. The two published rates are real starting points, not the whole picture, and the fastest way to know what your specific shoot costs is to describe it and get a number back.
