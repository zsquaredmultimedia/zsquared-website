VIDEO PRODUCTION IN LAS VEGAS

What "video production" actually covers, which format your shoot needs, and why doing this work in Las Vegas specifically changes the equation.

Kyle ZuelkeJuly 21, 2026 · 6 min read

Video production in Las Vegas covers more ground than the phrase suggests. It can mean a same-day ENG shoot with one camera operator and a shotgun mic. It can mean a five-camera switched broadcast running fiber out of Allegiant Stadium. It can mean a documentary crew embedded with a team for a season, or a ninety-second brand video shot on the Strip before sunrise. All of it falls under "video production," and figuring out which kind you actually need is the first real decision on any project — before gear, before budget, before a single call sheet gets built.

What "video production" actually covers

Strip away the marketing language and video production breaks down into a handful of formats, each with its own crew size, gear list, and turnaround time. ENG (electronic news gathering) is the fast, lean end — one or two people, a camera, and a plan to be in and out in a few hours. EFP (electronic field production) is more deliberate: more setup, more lighting, sometimes multiple cameras, built around a shot list rather than reacting to whatever happens. Multi-camera production layers in a switcher, syncing several feeds into one live or live-to-tape show. Documentary work is long-form and relationship-driven, often shot over weeks or months. Brand and social video is shorter and more stylized, built for a specific platform and a specific audience. Most production companies specialize in one or two of these. Z Squared runs all of them out of one Las Vegas-based crew roster, which matters when a project needs to shift formats midstream.

ENG crews: the fast, lean option

ENG is where most video production requests actually start, even when the client doesn't know the term yet. An interview, a set of b-roll, a corporate update, a quick news hit — these don't need a lighting truck or a call sheet a week in advance. They need a camera operator who shows up with the right gear and knows how to read a room. A standard ENG crew with camera, audio kit, and lighting starts at $1,950 per 10-hour day in Las Vegas, and full ENG/EFP packages with an audio tech and additional gear start at $3,000 per 10-hour day. See the ENG/EFP services page for what's included at each tier.

Multi-camera production for live events

Anything that airs live, or needs to feel live, usually calls for multiple cameras cutting to a single program feed. Z Squared runs multi-cam shows with up to 14 cameras, switched through an ATEM Constellation, NewTek Tricaster, or vMix depending on what the distribution requires. This is the format behind conventions, awards shows, corporate general sessions, and residency-style events on the Strip — anywhere a director needs to cut between angles in real time instead of assembling everything in post. It's also the format with the most upfront planning: camera positions, cable runs, and a signal path all get worked out before the crew ever touches gear. More detail lives on the multi-cam services page.

Documentary and long-form work

Documentary production runs on a different clock entirely. Instead of one day, it's built around access over time — following a subject, a team, or an organization long enough that the story reveals itself rather than getting forced into a script. Z Squared's documentary and sit-down work has included UFC projects, which is a useful example of what this format actually demands: patience, trust with the subject, and a crew that can shoot cleanly without disrupting whatever's actually happening in the room. The documentary services page covers how these projects typically get structured.

Brand and social video

Not every project is broadcast-scale. Brand and social video is shorter, more visually driven, and built for a specific channel — a 30-second spot, a series of vertical clips, a sizzle reel for a sales team. The crew is often smaller than an ENG shoot but the shot list is tighter, because every second has to earn its place. It's a different discipline from news-style shooting, and it's worth asking a prospective crew directly whether they've done this kind of work before hiring them for it. More on this format is on the brand and social page.

Why Las Vegas is a different production market

Las Vegas isn't a generic market with a few casinos in the background — it's one of the densest live-event cities in the country. Allegiant Stadium, T-Mobile Arena, Michelob Ultra Arena, the Las Vegas Convention Center, and the UFC Apex all sit within a short drive of each other, and all of them host national broadcasts on a regular schedule. It's crewed Super Bowl LVIII at Allegiant Stadium for NFL Network, NBA All-Star coverage, a Boston Celtics media day, and ESPN's WNBA Aces coverage, in addition to ongoing work with UFC, WWE, and the Las Vegas Raiders. What does video production cost in Las Vegas?

Pricing depends entirely on format. A standard ENG crew starts at $1,950 per 10-hour day; a full ENG/EFP package with an audio tech and extra gear starts at $3,000 per 10-hour day. Live transmission and multi-cam shows are quoted per event, since camera count, crew size, and signal requirements vary too much for a flat rate.
